Getting Started

Know Your Phone Camera

"Every great film starts not with an expensive camera — it starts with understanding the one you already have. Your phone is a precision instrument. Learn it before you buy anything."
Grid Lines ONSettings → Camera → Grid — Always keep this on. It shows you the rule of thirds.
Lock Focus + ExposureTap and hold on your subject until you see AE/AF LOCK. This stops the camera from auto-adjusting mid-shot.
Shoot in Pro / Manual ModeUse Pro mode if available. Set ISO, shutter speed, and white balance yourself for full control.
Resolution & FPSAlways record at the highest resolution. For cinematic feel: 24fps. For slow-motion: 60fps or higher.
Core Concept

The Exposure Triangle

☀️
ISO — Light Sensitivity
Controls how sensitive the sensor is to light. Low ISO (100–400) = clean image in bright light. High ISO (800+) = brighter in dark, but grainy. For phones: keep ISO as low as possible.
⏱️
Shutter Speed — Motion Blur
The 180° Rule: Set your shutter speed to double your frame rate. Shooting 24fps? Use 1/50 shutter. This gives natural motion blur — the look of real cinema.
🔭
Aperture — Depth of Field
Phone aperture is mostly fixed. But you can simulate shallow depth of field by moving the subject far from the background, and getting physically close with your phone lens.
Quick Settings Checklist
🎬
Turn Off HDR Video
HDR video can look unnatural and is hard to edit. Shoot in standard color profile (SDR) for easier, more cinematic editing.
Settings
🌡️
Fix White Balance
Auto white balance shifts colour during a shot. In Pro mode, lock the Kelvin value. Daylight ≈ 5500K. Tungsten light ≈ 3200K.
Colour
📱
Use Rear Camera
The rear camera always has a bigger sensor and better optics than the selfie camera. Always prefer rear camera unless you have no other option.

Visual Language

The Art of Framing

"Framing is not about what you show — it's about what you choose to leave out. Every edge of the frame is a decision. Make it intentional."
Three Golden Rules
1
Rule of Thirds

Divide your frame into a 3×3 grid. Place your subject on one of the four intersection points — not the dead center. This creates tension, balance, and visual interest.

2
Headroom & Lookroom

Leave a small gap above your subject's head (headroom). If they're looking left, leave more space in that direction (lookroom). Too much or too little feels wrong.

3
Lead Lines

Use roads, fences, shadows, or any natural lines in the scene to guide the viewer's eye toward your subject. These are free — they exist everywhere around you.

Practical Framing Tips
🚫
Avoid Centre-Lock
Placing everything dead-centre looks amateur. Break the symmetry unless it's intentional (like a Wes Anderson shot).
🌿
Use Foreground
Put leaves, a wall edge, or any object slightly in front of the lens to add depth. This makes phone footage look filmic.
🔳
Frame Within Frame
Use doors, windows, and archways to create a natural frame around your subject. Instant cinematic look.
📐
Horizon Line
Keep horizon straight unless a Dutch angle (tilted) is intentional. A crooked horizon looks like a mistake, not art.
🔍
Move Physically
Never zoom on a phone — it's just digital crop. Instead, physically walk closer. You get better quality and naturally shallow depth.
Field Guide
✓ Do This
  • Use the phone grid overlay at all times
  • Place subjects on rule-of-thirds points
  • Use natural frames: doors, windows, trees
  • Shoot wide first, then close up for coverage
  • Check background — remove clutter before rolling
  • Shoot in landscape (16:9) unless making Reels
  • Use leading lines in the environment
✗ Avoid This
  • Never use digital zoom — walk closer instead
  • Avoid dead-centre framing unless intentional
  • Don't cut at joints (knees, wrists, neck)
  • Avoid busy backgrounds that compete with subject
  • Never shoot with the sun directly behind the subject
  • Don't tilt the horizon by accident
  • Avoid fast whip-pans — they look shaky and cheap

Visual Grammar

Shot Types & Camera Angles

"Shots are your vocabulary. A director who knows only one shot size is like a writer who knows only one word. Learn the language — then break it."
EWS
Extreme Wide
Shows massive environment. Subject tiny. Used for establishing scenes or isolation.
WS
Wide Shot
Full body visible. Establishes character in location. Great for action.
MS
Medium Shot
Waist up. Most common conversational shot. Safe and natural.
CU
Close Up
Face fills frame. Shows emotion. One of the most powerful shots in filmmaking.
ECU
Extreme Close Up
Single detail — eye, hand, lips. Creates intense drama. Use with purpose.
LA
Low Angle
Camera below subject. Makes them look powerful, threatening, or heroic.
HA
High Angle
Camera above subject. Makes them look small, vulnerable, or observed.
OTS
Over the Shoulder
See one person from behind, face of the other. Standard for dialogue scenes.

Zero Budget Gear

Stabilisation Without a Gimbal

"Shaky footage is the fastest way to look amateur. But you don't need a ₹15,000 gimbal. Your body is a stabiliser — you just have to learn how to use it."
🧍
Soldier Stance
Body Technique
Feet shoulder-width apart. Elbows tucked into your ribs. Hold phone with both hands. Breathe out before pressing record. This alone reduces 60% of shake.
🧱
Lean on Walls
Environmental
Press your back or shoulder against a wall, tree, or pole. You've turned your body into a tripod. Works amazingly for static or slow-pan shots.
📦
DIY Tripod
Household Object
Stack books, use a water bottle on its side, prop the phone against a cup or bag. Tape the phone if needed. Free, stable, and always available.
🚶
Walk Heel-to-Toe
Movement Tech
For tracking shots — walk slow, roll from heel to toe with each step. Bent knees act as shock absorbers. Your walk becomes a dolly move.
🔗
Sling Strap Trick
DIY Gear
Tie a string from the phone to your foot (step on it). Pull the string taut upward while holding the phone. Creates tension-based stabilisation. Like a poor-man's steadicam.
✂️
Edit in Stabilisation
Post Processing
CapCut and DaVinci Resolve both have free stabilisation tools. Apply after shooting. Always leave 10% extra frame around your shot for the software to work with.
Pro Technique
🪜
The Slider Trick
Place your phone on a smooth surface — a table, ironing board, or tile floor — and slide it slowly. Instant cinematic slider shot. Works even better with a soft cloth under the phone.
DIY Slider
🔄
Spin on Bottle Cap
Place the phone on a bottle cap or smooth surface and spin it slowly for a rotating wide shot. Perfect for product shots or dramatic reveals. Zero cost orbital move.
DIY Rotation

The Most Important Chapter

DIY Lighting Techniques

"Cinema is painting with light. Cinematographers spend 70% of their time lighting. With zero budget, you're not lighting-poor — you're lighting-creative. Window light alone has given us some of the greatest images in film history."
Core Lighting Sources — All Free
Window Light (Natural)
The most cinematic free light source. Directional, soft, and beautiful. Used in films from Bergman to Nolan.
  • Place subject at 45° to the window
  • Overcast days = softest, most flattering light
  • Direct sunlight = harsh shadows (use curtain as diffuser)
  • Shoot in the morning or late afternoon (golden hour)
Smartphone Torch
A second phone's torch can be a key light, rim light, or dramatic fill. Small, powerful, and always in your pocket.
  • Bounce off white wall for soft fill light
  • Direct on face from side = noir dramatic look
  • From below = horror/thriller effect
  • Use tissue paper over torch to diffuse and soften
DIY Reflector
A reflector bounces existing light to fill in shadows. Professional ones cost ₹800. Yours costs ₹0.
  • White cardboard = soft neutral fill
  • Silver aluminium foil on cardboard = stronger fill
  • Gold foil = warm golden fill (great for portraits)
  • Hold at 45° opposite the main light source
Ceiling Bounce Light
Aim a lamp or torch at the white ceiling instead of directly at the subject. Light bounces down soft and wide — looks natural and professional.
  • Works only with white or light-coloured ceilings
  • Any household desk lamp works
  • Removes harsh shadows under nose and eyes
  • Great for indoor interview or monologue scenes
The Classic Setup

3-Point Lighting — Zero Budget Version

Professional studios use three lights. You can recreate this setup using household items. This single technique will make your footage look 10× more professional.

💡
Key Light — Main Source
Your primary light. Placed 45° to one side of your subject.

Free version: Window light. Or a desk lamp aimed at subject. This lights the face and creates the main shadows.
Most Important
🪞
Fill Light — Shadow Softener
Placed opposite the key light. Softens harsh shadows without removing them.

Free version: White cardboard reflector or a second phone torch on low brightness.
Balance
🔆
Back/Rim Light — Separation
Placed behind the subject, aimed at the back of their head/shoulders. Creates a bright rim that separates subject from background.

Free version: Another phone torch or a lamp behind the chair.
Cinematic
Mood Through Light
🌑
Low Key — Thriller / Horror
Mostly dark frame with a single hard source (torch, lamp). High contrast. Deep shadows. Little to no fill light. Subject emerges from darkness. Single candle or phone torch works perfectly.
Dramatic
☀️
High Key — Happy / Neutral
Even, bright lighting with minimal shadows. Multiple soft sources. Use near a window on an overcast day. Bounce light off multiple white walls. Great for comedy, interviews, or documentary style.
Upbeat
🌅
Golden Hour — Warm & Cinematic
One hour after sunrise and one hour before sunset. The sun is low, soft, and warm orange. This light is free, naturally beautiful, and makes everything look like a film still. Plan your shoots around it.
Cinematic
🔵
Blue Hour — Cold & Moody
Just after sunset, the sky turns deep blue for 15–20 minutes. Perfect for psychological or mysterious scenes. No setup needed — just be at your location on time. This is free cinema-grade lighting.
Moody

Often Ignored, Always Important

Audio — The Hidden Half of Cinema

"People will forgive shaky footage. They will not forgive bad audio. A viewer turns off a film with poor sound in 10 seconds. Good audio makes people believe everything else is professional."
🌬️
Wind Noise
The phone mic is extremely sensitive to wind. Even slight breeze ruins dialogue. Shoot indoors or block wind with your hand or cloth.
🔁
Echo & Reverb
Big empty rooms create echo. Cushions, curtains, clothes, and blankets absorb sound. Record in small, furnished rooms whenever possible.
🎙️
Get Close to Source
The closer the mic is to the speaker, the better the audio. Move the phone as close as possible even if you'll reframe in editing.
🧣
DIY Windscreen
Wrap a soft piece of cloth lightly over the mic hole at the bottom of the phone. Reduces wind noise by 50–70%. Free windscreen.
🎧
Earphone Mic Trick
Plug in your earphones with a built-in mic and hide the wire under clothing. Instant lavalier microphone — comes free with most phones.
🔇
ADR — Re-record Later
If location audio is bad, record the dialogue separately in a quiet room and sync in editing. This is called ADR — used in every major film production.
Audio Checklist
✓ Good Audio Habits
  • Always monitor audio with earphones while recording
  • Record a 10-second room tone (silence) on every location
  • Use earphone mic as a free lav mic hidden in clothing
  • Record in small rooms with soft surfaces
  • Test audio before the main take
  • Clap once before rolling — makes sync easier in edit
✗ Common Audio Mistakes
  • Don't shoot near roads, fans, or ACs if avoidable
  • Never hold the phone mic near your hands — finger noise
  • Don't rely only on post-processing to fix bad audio
  • Avoid shooting in tiled bathrooms or bare concrete rooms
  • Never record dialogue in open fields without windscreen
  • Don't forget to check audio settings before each scene

The Cinematographer's Final Rules
🎥
Watch Films Like a Cinematographer
Watch films with the sound off. Observe where the light comes from. Notice how shadows fall. Ask yourself: what shot is this? Why did they choose it? This is your free film school — every film you watch is a masterclass.
📝
Shot-list Before You Shoot
Sketch your shots before arriving on location. Know your wide, medium, and close-up for each scene. A 10-minute shot-list saves 2 hours of confused shooting. Preparation is the cheapest form of gear.
🌤️
Scout Your Location
Visit your location at the same time of day you'll be shooting. See where the light falls. Notice the natural lines and frames. Remove clutter from background. Location scouting turns a random place into a set.
🔁
Shoot. Review. Improve.
Your first 10 films will teach you more than any book. But only if you watch them critically. What looks shaky? What looks dark? What works? Review every frame. The feedback loop is your real film school.
"Light is cheap. Knowledge is free. Excuses are expensive."
